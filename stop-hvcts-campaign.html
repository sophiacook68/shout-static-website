Stop Home-Owners' Unfair Tax

SHOUT is a community-led campaign against the High Value Council Tax Surcharge ("HVCTS") — highlighting the unforeseen consequences on local communities.

Tax experts have said that the tax will not raise much revenue and cannot achieve the claimed revenue. It will be applied to 0.4–1% of properties in England. SHOUT believes the tax must be abandoned or at least modified. You can help too by volunteering or donating.

The Policy

What is the High Value Council Tax Surcharge?

People in the UK already pay the highest property taxes in the world. High-value homes pay between 12–17% Stamp Duty on purchase and more council tax than lower-value homes. The HVCTS adds yet another annual levy on top.

The High Value Council Tax Surcharge — announced in the Autumn 2025 Budget and informally known as the "Mansion Tax" — proposes an additional annual levy on residential properties valued at £2 million or above, based on 2026 valuations, coming into effect in April 2028.

The stratospheric level of London house prices results from the failure of successive governments to build enough housing. Ordinary home-owners are being wrongly punished. The creep effect will mean the surcharge will soon apply to all properties in London and many beyond.

What triggers the charge?

Properties valued at £2 million or above in the 2026 valuation exercise — regardless of the owner's income or financial circumstances.

When does it start?

April 2028 — two years after the 2026 valuation exercise on which it is based.

Is it recurring?

Yes. Unlike Stamp Duty, paid once on purchase, the HVCTS is charged every year the property remains above threshold.

Does it account for income?

No. The charge is entirely blind to the owner's financial position. A retired pensioner on a fixed income faces the same bill as a high earner.

Why We Oppose It

What's wrong with the HVCTS?

The HVCTS is neither rational nor fair and would be complex and inefficient to administer. Our taxation system should ensure fairness, simplicity, certainty and efficiency. The HVCTS fails on every count.

It is not an effective tool to raise tax from the wealthy

  • A large country house may not be liable whereas a modest London flat may be. The majority of properties affected will be in London and the South East — making the HVCTS in effect a regional tax on one part of England.
  • It is a yearly cash payment on a home which generates no income to pay for it. No similar charge is made on other types of wealth, including true luxuries such as boats, art and second homes.
  • It takes no account of an individual's ability to pay the tax.
  • It would not account for net property wealth — it ignores the mortgage or other borrowing secured against the home.
  • It does not take account of the costs of upkeep.

It is difficult and costly to administer

  • The Valuation Office Agency (VOA) will conduct a targeted valuation exercise in 2026 — a costly, resource-intensive undertaking. Disputed valuations will generate significant legal and administrative costs, and revaluations every five years create further ongoing uncertainty.
  • Properties will be valued in 2026 but the HVCTS does not take effect until April 2028 — two years in which values may change significantly. Revaluations occur only every five years, meaning the surcharge may bear no relation to current market reality for extended periods.

The revenue would be negligible

  • The £430m per year projected yield is less than 0.05% of total government receipts — a rounding error in public finances — yet it will generate significant compliance costs, legal disputes, market distortions, and hardship for asset-rich but cash-poor homeowners.
  • Property values may fall in response to the new charge, reducing Stamp Duty Land Tax and Inheritance Tax receipts. A significant portion of the HVCTS yield could be offset by losses elsewhere in the tax system.

It is a political tool

The "council tax surcharge" rebrand is a cosmetic device designed to suggest a "fairness" agenda. In practice, the HVCTS is a postcode-based wealth tax targeted overwhelmingly on London and the South East, cynically designed to win votes by encouraging envy and pitting different areas of the country against each other.

The correct remedy is a wholesale reform of the outdated 1991 council tax banding system — not a politically motivated surcharge layered on top of it.

Context & Warning

The Mansion Tax will harm homeowners and communities

Warning: threshold creep. When the Government introduced the Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings ("ATED") in 2012, it applied to properties worth over £2 million. Within a year this was lowered to £1 million; the year after to £500,000. All homeowners and residents should be concerned — this new tax may later be imposed on their home.

A Mansion Tax will effectively be an eviction order, forcing many residents to sell. Many who bought their homes years ago could not afford to buy at today's prices. They do not have the income to pay a swingeing annual levy on an asset that produces no income and requires cash for maintenance. These homeowners — often elderly or with children in school or college — are "asset rich and cash poor."

Any money raised will be siphoned out of neighbourhoods into the Treasury, removing disposable income from local businesses, communities and charities. Some estimates suggest it will result in a net loss to the Treasury once the effect on Stamp Duty, Inheritance Tax, VAT and Income Tax are taken into account.

The Mansion Tax is a wealth tax. Chancellor Denis Healey attempted one in 1976 and abandoned it, admitting it was "impossible to draft one which would yield enough revenue to be worth the administrative cost and political hassle."
Wealth taxes have been abandoned across Europe: Austria, Denmark and Germany in 1997; Finland and Luxembourg in 2006; Sweden in 2007; Iceland in 2014. In 2014, President Hollande was forced to reform the French wealth tax.
The tax burden on higher-value properties has already risen enormously since 2010. The tax paid on a £2.5m house increased by over 400% between 2009 and 2015 alone — before subsequent SDLT surcharges were added. The HVCTS adds yet another layer of punitive double taxation.

UK assets have always been taxed on sale through Capital Gains Tax and Stamp Duty — reflecting the well-established principle that tax should only be raised from cash available on sale or purchase. The HVCTS ignores these fundamental, long-established principles.

Timeline

Key dates to watch

Autumn 2025

HVCTS announced in the Budget

The High Value Council Tax Surcharge is announced. SHOUT is established to campaign against it.

2026

Property valuation exercise

The VOA conducts a targeted valuation exercise. Properties valued at £2m+ fall within scope. This is the critical window for challenge and lobbying — the time to act is now.

2026–2027

Legislative passage

Legislation must pass through Parliament. MPs can be influenced. Write to yours before this becomes law.

April 2028

HVCTS charges commence

If enacted, annual surcharge bills begin. The first five-year revaluation cycle starts. Threshold creep becomes a real risk for all homeowners.
